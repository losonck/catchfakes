The Rolex Air-King, particularly the references 126900 and 116900, stands as an emblem of aviation heritage and precision engineering. However, its popularity has also made it a target for counterfeiters. With prices for genuine models reaching several thousand pounds, the financial risk of purchasing a fake is substantial. The market is flooded with counterfeits that can deceive even seasoned collectors. This guide will arm you with the knowledge to authenticate these specific references, ensuring your investment is sound.

## Dial Details and Printing

The dial of the Rolex Air-King 126900 and 116900 is one of the first places to check for authenticity. Genuine models feature a distinctive black dial with a combination of large 3, 6, and 9 numerals and a prominent minute scale for navigational time readings. The Rolex crown at 12 o'clock is sharply defined. The 'Rolex' and 'Oyster Perpetual' text should be crisp and perfectly aligned. Counterfeit dials often have misaligned text or uneven printing, which is a clear red flag.

## Movement Verification

The Rolex Air-King 126900 houses the calibre 3230, while the 116900 is powered by the calibre 3131. Both movements are renowned for their precision and reliability. Authentic movements feature Rolex's proprietary Parachrom hairspring, which is blue in colour. Counterfeit models may use generic movements that lack the intricate finishing and decoration found in genuine Rolex calibres. If possible, have the movement inspected by a professional to confirm these details.

## Caseback and Engravings

Rolex Air-King models have a solid caseback with no engravings visible from the outside. If you encounter a model with an engraved caseback, it is likely a counterfeit. Inside, the caseback should have precise engravings that include the model number and the Rolex name. These engravings are laser-etched with precision, which counterfeiters often fail to replicate accurately.

## Bracelet and Clasp Codes

The Air-King 126900 and 116900 are fitted with the Oyster bracelet, reference 72600. The bracelet should feel robust and well-constructed, with no sharp edges. The clasp will have the Rolex crown logo and should operate smoothly. Inside the clasp, you'll find the bracelet code, which should match the production year of the watch. Counterfeits may have mismatched or missing codes.

## Rehaut Engraving

The rehaut, or the inner bezel, of the Air-King should have the Rolex name engraved repeatedly around it, along with the serial number at 6 o'clock. This engraving is done with precision and should be perfectly aligned with the dial markers. Counterfeits often have poorly executed engravings that are misaligned or too shallow.

## Weight and Sound

A genuine Rolex Air-King has a significant weight due to its high-quality materials. It should feel substantial on the wrist. Additionally, the ticking of a Rolex is smooth and almost silent, thanks to its high-beat movement. Counterfeits often feel lighter and may produce a louder ticking sound, indicating a lower quality movement.

## Red Flags at a Glance

- Misaligned dial text
- Engraved caseback
- Missing or mismatched bracelet codes
- Poorly executed rehaut engraving
- Lightweight feel

## FAQ

### What movement does the Rolex Air-King 126900 use?

The Rolex Air-King 126900 uses the calibre 3230 movement.

### Are there engravings on the caseback of a genuine Air-King?

No, genuine Air-King models have a solid caseback with no external engravings.

### How can I check the authenticity of the bracelet?

Check for the bracelet code inside the clasp and ensure it matches the watch's production year.

## Final Word

Authenticating a Rolex Air-King, especially the 126900 and 116900 references, requires attention to detail and knowledge of specific features. While this guide provides a comprehensive overview, consulting with a professional or obtaining a second opinion can offer additional assurance. Protect your investment by ensuring every aspect of the watch aligns with what is expected from a genuine Rolex.
